Developers and their jobs

The various chaotic hiring-and-firing events over at the White House in the past few days have got me thinking about staff turnover in development teams.

A certain amount of staff turnover is essential for a healthy team dynamic. I’ve worked in at at least one company where a significant number of “lifers” had worked there for 20 years or more. That’s no bad thing in itself — an experienced hand is a valuable asset. But when close to everyone has been there forever then a team can become set in its ways and resistant to positive change.

Similarly, you don’t want a revolving door situation where no-one stays for more than a few weeks. Too much knowledge and continuity is lost as people come and go.

If our turnover is fairly stable and we’re at a point where a new person starts, say, every 6–12 months, then we end up with an interesting problem on our hands. Joining those kinds of teams is a real pain.

It’s the same class of problem as those rarely-executed fiddly technical tasks. Clearing up temporary files, flushing a cache, consolidating backups. Nobody takes the time to improve the process because it happens so rarely.

I’ve been in this situation, and I’ve been asked to document the on-boarding process for the next person. And this is the single worst thing I could be asked to do on my first day.

It seems logical — who better to understand what the new guy needs than the new guy?
